The apartment locating industry runs at roughly 20% margins if you're doing well. You make a commission when a renter signs a lease at a building you referred them to. The commission is meaningful — usually one month's rent. But the cost of acquiring, qualifying, and converting that renter eats almost everything.

Human agents cost money. They need desks, managers, training, benefits. They work eight hours a day and spend half of that on leads that will never convert. They can't work 3,000 conversations simultaneously. They get tired. They make mistakes. They quit.

The Masa Son thesis — infinite revenue at near-zero marginal cost — isn't theoretical for me. I've been living it. When your lead qualification is automated, your inventory matching is automated, your follow-up cadence is automated, your document collection is automated, your compliance checking is automated — what's left? Phone calls and apartment tours. Two things that require either a real voice or a physical body.

Everything else is machine. And machines don't cost per-unit at scale.

We hit 97% contribution margins not by cutting corners. Not by offering a worse product. By removing the humans from processes that never needed humans in the first place. The renter gets faster responses, more accurate matches, 24/7 availability. The building gets higher conversion rates. We get margins that are structurally impossible for a human-operated competitor.

This is what happens when you apply internet economics to an offline industry. It's not disruption. It's physics.
