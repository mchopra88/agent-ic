Every realtor does the same thing. They take requirements from a customer — budget, location, move-in date, pet policy, parking. They query their inventory — MLS, personal contacts, building relationships. They present options. They handle objections. They close. The entire job description fits inside a system prompt.

I didn't arrive at this insight from the technology side. I arrived at it from the operations side, after years of managing human agents who did this work manually. The best agents weren't creative. They were systematic. The worst agents weren't bad at relationships — they were bad at data retrieval. The job was never about charm. It was about information throughput.

Once you see it, you can't unsee it. An insurance broker is the same pattern with different inventory. A quoting desk for industrial distribution is the same pattern with SKUs instead of apartments. A physician dispatch service is the same pattern with availability slots instead of floor plans.

The question was never "can AI do this?" The question was always "why are we paying humans to be slow, inconsistent databases with legs?"
