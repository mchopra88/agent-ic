Most teams have one feedback loop. They do retrospectives. What happened this sprint? What went well? What didn't? This is the retro loop and it operates at the level of individual events. It's necessary and insufficient.

The macro loop operates at the level of patterns. Not "what happened in this conversation" but "what's happening across 30,000 conversations this week?" Are qualification rates trending down? Is a particular building generating complaints? Are follow-up cadences converting differently in Dallas than Chicago? The macro loop requires data infrastructure that most organizations don't have because they're still manually reviewing individual cases.

The meta loop is the one nobody talks about. It operates at the level of the system itself. Not "is the agent performing well?" but "is the system that builds and improves the agent getting better at building and improving agents?" Are your deployment pipelines faster than last month? Are your guardrails catching more edge cases? Is your institutional knowledge — encoded in CLAUDE.md files, session memory, hooks, skills — growing in a way that compounds?

Most teams never get past retro. The ones that reach macro have a structural advantage. The ones that reach meta have a compounding advantage. The system that improves the system that improves the system — that's where operating leverage becomes exponential rather than linear.

I didn't design these three loops deliberately. I discovered them by building alone for seven years and noticing that the improvements were happening at different altitudes. The retro loop fixes today's bug. The macro loop fixes this quarter's pattern. The meta loop fixes next year's architecture. You need all three.
