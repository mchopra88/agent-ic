For most of the last few years, I believed I was unemployable.

Not in the dramatic way. In the quiet, 2 AM way — the way you believe something that you never say out loud because saying it would make it real. I'd left a career that made sense on paper: Groupon through the IPO, Google, the Slack liquidity event, venture partner, board seats, the whole résumé arc. I'd traded it for a bunker in Chicago where I built AI systems for an industry nobody in tech takes seriously.

Apartment locating. Try explaining that at a dinner party.

The doubt wasn't about the business. The business worked. Revenue came in. The systems ran. The margins were extraordinary. The doubt was about me — about whether the skills I'd built in the bunker had any value outside the bunker. I didn't have a CS degree. I didn't have a GitHub profile that looked like an engineer's. I had seven years of solo building in an industry that doesn't show up on any VC's radar.

My dad died just before the world turned upside down. The last match I was at before Covid shut everything down, before everything changed, was Liverpool against Manchester United at Anfield. Salah scored — one of those goals that makes 54,000 people lose their minds simultaneously. Straight from a Becker assist. YNWA.

Walk on, walk on, with hope in your heart.

I didn't know that would be the last normal moment for a long time. That the world would close, that I'd lose my father, that I'd spend the next years building alone in a way that felt more like an act of will than a business strategy. Vishwamitra bent reality with his will. That's what it felt like — not talent, not strategy, just the stubborn refusal to stop building.

The Bhagavad Gita puts Arjuna on a battlefield, paralyzed by doubt. Krishna doesn't tell him the outcome will be good. He tells him that action without attachment to outcome is the only path. You do the work. You build the system. You deploy at 2 AM. You don't get to know if it matters. You just keep going.

Kipling wrote it too:

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.95rem; line-height:1.9; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0; font-style:italic;">
If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew
    To serve your turn long after they are gone,
And so hold on when there is nothing in you
    Except the Will which says to them: "Hold on!"
</pre>

That was it. For years. Hold on. Build. Ship. Fix what breaks. Don't look up. Don't check if anyone's watching. The machine runs whether I believe in it or not.

About three months ago, something broke through. I was showing someone the architecture — the tmux sessions, the CLAUDE.md files, the hook systems, the adversarial testing pipelines, the session memory architecture — and I watched their face change. Not impressed in the polite way. Impressed in the "wait, one person built this?" way.

Then it happened again. And again. With technical people, with investors, with operators trying to figure out how to deploy AI into their own organizations. The same pause. The same recalibration. I showed Eric and Eddie the full view — the Claude Code evolution, the commit history, the CLAUDE.md architecture, how I push more code solo than their engineering teams — and the response was visceral.

The death of doubt wasn't a single moment. It was the accumulation of evidence that the skills I'd built — orchestrating AI agents at production scale, building autonomous overnight operations, collapsing entire business functions into code — aren't niche. They're the exact thing every company is trying to figure out. I just happened to figure it out early, alone, in a bunker, for an industry nobody was watching.

"Do you want to be worth $600,000 to someone else, or do you want to own the machine?"

I know the answer now. I didn't for a long time.

For Ved and Reya: a machine that runs while their father sleeps. The Gayatri Mantra of residential leasing — something that keeps working after you stop. Not a job to inherit. A system.
