For most of the last few years, I believed I was unemployable.

Not in the dramatic, feel-sorry-for-me way. In the quiet, 2 AM way — the way you believe something that you never say out loud because saying it would make it real. I'd left a career that made sense on paper: Groupon, IPOs, venture, board seats, the whole résumé arc. I'd traded it for a bunker in Chicago where I built AI systems for an industry nobody in tech takes seriously.

Apartment locating. Try explaining that at a dinner party.

The doubt wasn't about the business. The business worked. Revenue came in. The systems ran. The margins were extraordinary. The doubt was about me — about whether the skills I'd built in the bunker had any value outside the bunker. I didn't have a CS degree. I didn't have a GitHub profile that looked like an engineer's. I had seven years of solo building in an industry that doesn't show up on any VC's radar.

About three months ago, something broke. I was showing someone the architecture — the tmux sessions, the CLAUDE.md files, the hook systems, the adversarial testing pipelines, the session memory architecture — and I watched their face change. Not impressed in the polite way. Impressed in the "wait, one person built this?" way.

Then it happened again. And again. With technical people, with investors, with operators trying to figure out how to deploy AI into their own organizations. The same pause. The same recalibration.

The death of doubt wasn't a single moment. It was the accumulation of evidence that the skills I'd built — orchestrating AI systems at production scale, building autonomous overnight operations, pushing more code solo than engineering teams — aren't niche. They're the exact thing every company is trying to figure out. I just happened to figure it out early, alone, in a bunker, for an industry nobody was watching.
