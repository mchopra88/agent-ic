Ben Thompson wrote the piece that changed how I think about my own company. The argument: Amazon didn't set out to build AWS. Amazon built logistics and compute infrastructure to serve itself, then realized the infrastructure was the real product. The bookstore was the first customer. The world was the market.

I read that and recognized my own trajectory. I built AI systems to run my apartment locating business. Voice agents, SMS engines, data pipelines, inventory databases, qualification systems. I built them because I needed them. I built them because the humans I hired couldn't do the work fast enough, consistently enough, cheaply enough.

Then people started asking about the technology. Partners, investors, operators in adjacent industries. "How does your system work?" "Can we use that?" "What would it cost to deploy that into our portfolio company?"

For years, I said no. The systems needed to prove themselves in production first, at real scale, over years — not months. They have. So the question becomes: am I a brokerage that happens to have technology, or a technology studio that happens to operate a brokerage?

The answer changes everything about what this company is worth.
