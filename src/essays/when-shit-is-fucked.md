In 2021, a few hundred million dollars of institutional capital followed into Chicago's South Side apartment market. The headline deal: Pangea Properties — 7,500 units across the South Side — sold to a new ownership group. The real number, which didn't make the press but I know because I know the principals personally, was closer to a billion dollars.

When you spend a billion dollars, you spend it because you expect the asset to perform. Specifically: you expect vacancy to stay low, rents to climb with inflation, and the operating cost structure to support the debt service on a leveraged acquisition. That's the institutional real estate model. It works when the macro environment cooperates.

Three things happened that broke the model:

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.8rem; line-height:1.6; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0;">
What happened to a billion dollars in South Side real estate:

  1. COVID (2020-2021)
     → Eviction moratoriums
     → Rent collection collapsed
     → Deferred maintenance compounded
     → Tenants who would have been replaced stayed,
       often without paying

  2. Interest rates (2022-2024)
     → Fed funds rate: 0% → 5.5%
     → Debt service on leveraged acquisitions
       roughly doubled
     → Refinancing became impossible at original terms
     → The math that justified the purchase price
       no longer worked

  3. Brandon Johnson (2023-present)
     → Boosted Section 8 voucher values significantly
     → Policy goal: give voucher holders access
       to better neighborhoods
     → Result: voucher tenants left the South Side
       for the North Side and suburbs
     → South Side buildings went from ~5% vacancy
       to ~20% vacancy in 18 months
     → The tenants who WERE paying (via voucher)
       are now paying somewhere else
</pre>

Number 3 was the hardest. COVID was temporary. Interest rates are cyclical. But a policy change that structurally redirects your tenant base to competing neighborhoods — that's a paradigm shift. The buildings that were 95% occupied and generating predictable cash flow are now 80% occupied and bleeding.

A billion dollars on the line. Vacancy climbing. Leasing teams that can't retain agents because the success rate on South Side leasing is low enough that commission-only agents starve. Nobody wants to do this work. The conversion rates are terrible. The tenant pool is difficult. The buildings need work. The neighborhoods are hard.

And I'm the guy who built his entire infrastructure for exactly this situation.

## Built for Low-Success Funnels

My system was built for dead leads. Not metaphorically — literally. The entire architecture was designed to process leads that most brokerages would discard.

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.8rem; line-height:1.6; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0;">
Traditional brokerage lead economics:

  Lead → Human agent → Qualify → Tour → Close
  Success rate: 2-5% (lead to closed deal)
  Cost per lead: $15-$25 in agent time
  At 2% conversion, cost per deal: $750-$1,250
  Works when deal values justify the cost.
  Breaks when deal values are low (cheap apartments)
  or conversion is low (difficult markets).

My system:

  Lead → AI qualification → AI conversation →
  → AI matching → Tour (human) → Close
  Success rate: similar 2-5%
  Cost per lead: fractions of a cent
  At 2% conversion, cost per deal: ~$2-$5
  Works at ANY deal value.
  Works at ANY conversion rate.
  The economics don't break because the cost
  structure doesn't scale with volume.

  The system that works on $2,000/month luxury leads
  also works on $800/month South Side leads.
  The system that works at 5% conversion
  also works at 1% conversion.
  The machine doesn't care about the success rate.
  It processes everything.
</pre>

No South Side landlord was paying locator fees before I created the market. I'm not exaggerating. The locator model — where a renter goes to a broker and the broker earns a commission from the building for placing the tenant — existed on the North Side and in Dallas and in other high-value markets. It didn't exist on the South Side because the unit economics didn't support human agents at those price points. $800/month rent. 50% of first month as commission. $400 per placement. That doesn't pay for the agent's time at traditional cost structures.

At my cost structure, $400 per placement is extraordinary. The cost to acquire and convert that lead is single digits. The margin is over 95%. The math works at price points that no human-staffed brokerage can touch.

That's why I get the call when shit is fucked.

## There Is No Finish Line

The mistake people make about leasing — and about most operational businesses — is thinking there's a finish line. Build the ad, generate the leads, close the deals, collect the commissions. Done.

There is no done. Every step has a next step, and every next step has a failure mode:

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.8rem; line-height:1.6; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0;">
The infinite funnel:

  Post the building on ILS platforms
    → Are the photos good enough?
    → Is the pricing current?
    → Did the listing syndicate correctly?

  Generate leads from the listings
    → Are the leads real? (bot filtering)
    → Are they in the right market?
    → Do they match the building's requirements?

  Start the conversation
    → Did the AI qualify correctly?
    → Did it present the right buildings?
    → Did it handle the objections?

  Book the tour
    → Did the renter confirm?
    → Does the key work? (half the time: no)
    → Is the unit actually available?
    → Did anyone show up?

  After the tour
    → What did they say?
    → Did they like it?
    → What's their timeline?
    → Do they need to see more?

  Application
    → Did they submit everything?
    → Income verification?
    → Credit check?
    → Prior evictions?
    → Cosigner needed?

  Approval and lease signing
    → Building manager responsive?
    → Lease terms correct?
    → Move-in date set?
    → First month + deposit collected?

  After move-in
    → Did they actually move in?
    → Commission invoice submitted?
    → Building paid the invoice?
    → How long until they renew?

  EACH STEP can fail.
  EACH FAILURE is a revenue leak.
  There is no step where you can stop
  and say "we're done."
</pre>

This is what I mean when I say execution requires "absolute doggedness and belligerence." Not aggression — persistence. Dog with a bone. *Chhoroonga nahin.* I will not let go. Every step in the funnel is a potential revenue leak, and every leak compounds. If 50% of tours don't happen because the key doesn't work, you've lost half your revenue before the application process even starts. If 30% of approved applicants don't move in because the lease signing process is broken, you've done all that work for nothing.

The system has to be relentless at every step. Not just the sexy steps (AI conversation, matching algorithm). The boring steps too. Does the key work. Did the building manager respond. Was the invoice submitted. Is the commission correct. This is the work that nobody wants to do and that determines whether the business actually makes money.

## Not Cost Sensitive. Return Sensitive.

People sometimes think I'm cheap because I cut costs aggressively. I'm not cheap. I'll buy any tool, any subscription, any resource that generates returns. Claude Max, GKE clusters, Twilio enterprise — whatever it takes. The distinction isn't between spending and not spending. It's between spending with measurable returns and spending without them.

The engineering team cost $4,350/month and generated zero net revenue. The AI subscriptions cost $600/month and generate 97% margins on 30,000 conversations. The cost isn't the issue. The return is the issue.

When I tell the team "get Claude Max, get whatever you need" — I mean it. The tools pay for themselves in a single lease. One lease covers months of tooling costs. Why would I be cheap about tools? I'm not cost sensitive. I'm return sensitive. There's a critical difference, and most managers don't understand it. They cut tool budgets to save $200/month while losing $20,000/month in productivity.

The machine doesn't care about the neighborhood's reputation. The machine doesn't care about the conversion rate. The machine processes everything, at fractions of a cent, around the clock.

And when nobody else will do the work, I get the call.
