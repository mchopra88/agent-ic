Al Goldstein was born in Uzbekistan, immigrated to Skokie at age 8, and graduated from U of I with a finance degree in 2002. He built CashNetUSA — an online payday lender — and sold it for $265 million in 2006 at age 28. Then he took that capital and started buying apartments on Chicago's South and West Sides during the depths of the Great Recession. Some buildings went for less than $20,000 a unit. By 2022, Pangea Properties controlled approximately 7,500 units across 423 buildings — nearly all of them affordable at 60% of Area Median Income. The same demographic that CashNetUSA served now lived in his buildings.

In May 2022, Pangea entered a contract to sell the entire portfolio to Emerald Empire, a New York-based firm led by Moshe Wechsler. The deal closed in late 2022 for approximately $600 million — over $75,000 per unit, making it one of the largest multifamily transactions in Chicago history. It was financed through one of the largest Fannie Mae Structured Adjustable-Rate Mortgage loans of 2022, co-funded by Arbor Realty Trust and NewPoint Real Estate Capital.

I know the principals personally. The documented price was $600 million. But when you add the leveraged debt structure, the deferred maintenance obligations, the operating commitments, and the scale of what was being absorbed — the exposure was substantially larger than the headline number.

When you absorb that much exposure, you're betting the macro environment cooperates. Specifically: you expect vacancy to stay low, rents to climb with inflation, and the operating cost structure to support the debt service. That's the institutional real estate model. It works when the world cooperates.

Three things happened that broke the model:

Here is the critical correction to the narrative you hear in real estate circles: the story is NOT that Brandon Johnson's policies drove voucher holders to the North Side, emptying the South Side. The WBEZ data shows the opposite — vouchers are concentrating further into South and West Side neighborhoods, with a 25% decline in majority-white communities. The real story is simpler and uglier: overleveraged out-of-state investors bought buildings, skipped maintenance, tenants left, and the buildings started bleeding.

## The Emerald Empire Collapse

By August 2025, Emerald Empire's $430 million in Fannie Mae financing entered forbearance, with at least $125 million in loans under forbearance agreements. Multiple Emerald-owned properties were dragged through Chicago housing court for building code violations — rat abatement failures, fines of $500+. Emerald stopped paying distributions to investors and sent memos asking for additional capital.

This is what happens when you buy 7,500 units of aging South Side housing with floating-rate debt at peak pricing.

## The Pangea Fraud

There's another layer. In 2018, a former Pangea employee named Antoni Muhawi filed a whistleblower lawsuit alleging that Pangea had an established internal policy instructing staff to charge Section 8 tenants $150 more per month than market-rate tenants, then lie on CHA paperwork to hide the discrepancy. The suit alleged this cost CHA upward of $1 million per year in overpayments.

CHA found evidence in a 2015 investigation but dropped the matter before completing the analysis. After seven years of litigation, Pangea settled in August 2025 — terms undisclosed.

This is the market I operate in. Not the sanitized institutional version. The version where the largest landlord on the South Side was allegedly defrauding the housing authority, and the buyer who took over is now in forbearance on $430 million of debt, and the Brooklyn investors who piled in behind them are in foreclosure.

And I'm the guy who built his entire infrastructure for exactly this situation.

No South Side landlord was paying locator fees before I created the market. I'm not exaggerating. The locator model — where a renter goes to a broker and the broker earns a commission from the building for placing the tenant — existed on the North Side and in Dallas and in other high-value markets. It didn't exist on the South Side because the unit economics didn't support human agents at those price points. $800/month rent. 50% of first month as commission. $400 per placement. That doesn't pay for the agent's time at traditional cost structures.

At my cost structure, $400 per placement is extraordinary. The cost to acquire and convert that lead is single digits. The margin is over 95%. The math works at price points that no human-staffed brokerage can touch.

That's why I get the call when shit is fucked.

Every building that loses tenants faster than it can replace them is a building that needs leasing infrastructure.

The institutional investors from New York saw the Obama Presidential Center groundbreaking in South Shore in 2021 and thought it signaled a turning point. They paid $73,000 per unit in neighborhoods where rents don't support that basis. They didn't build the operational infrastructure to lease the buildings. They assumed the demand would come to them.

The demand didn't come. The buildings deteriorated. The tenants left. And now there are foreclosure filings across South Shore, and distressed portfolios hitting the market at fractions of what was paid, and nobody has the leasing funnel to fill them.

Except me.

## There Is No Finish Line

The mistake people make about leasing — and about most operational businesses — is thinking there's a finish line. Build the ad, generate the leads, close the deals, collect the commissions. Done.

There is no done. Every step has a next step, and every next step has a failure mode:

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.8rem; line-height:1.6; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0;">
The infinite funnel:

  Post the building on ILS platforms
    → Are the photos good enough?
    → Is the pricing current?
    → Did the listing syndicate correctly?

  Generate leads from the listings
    → Are the leads real? (bot filtering)
    → Are they in the right market?
    → Do they match the building's requirements?

  Start the conversation
    → Did the AI qualify correctly?
    → Did it present the right buildings?
    → Did it handle the objections?

  Book the tour
    → Did the renter confirm?
    → Does the key work? (half the time: no)
    → Is the unit actually available?
    → Did anyone show up?

  After the tour
    → What did they say?
    → Did they like it?
    → What's their timeline?
    → Do they need to see more?

  Application
    → Did they submit everything?
    → Income verification?
    → Credit check?
    → Prior evictions?
    → Cosigner needed?

  Approval and lease signing
    → Building manager responsive?
    → Lease terms correct?
    → Move-in date set?
    → First month + deposit collected?

  After move-in
    → Did they actually move in?
    → Commission invoice submitted?
    → Building paid the invoice?
    → How long until they renew?

  EACH STEP can fail.
  EACH FAILURE is a revenue leak.
  There is no step where you can stop
  and say "we're done."

This is what I mean when I say execution requires "absolute doggedness and belligerence." Not aggression — persistence. Dog with a bone. *Chhoroonga nahin.* I will not let go. Every step in the funnel is a potential revenue leak, and every leak compounds. If 50% of tours don't happen because the key doesn't work, you've lost half your revenue before the application process even starts. If 30% of approved applicants don't move in because the lease signing process is broken, you've done all that work for nothing.

The system has to be relentless at every step. Not just the sexy steps (AI conversation, matching algorithm). The boring steps too. Does the key work. Did the building manager respond. Was the invoice submitted. Is the commission correct. This is the work that nobody wants to do and that determines whether the business actually makes money.

## The Real Market

Here's the thing about this market that outsiders don't understand: the South Side isn't a monolith. CHA manages 47,000 vouchers serving 63,000+ families across all 77 community areas. South Shore alone has 3,487. The FY2025 Fair Market Rent for a 2-bedroom in the Chicago metro is $1,761 — that jumped 19% in a single year. Illinois made source of income a protected class on January 1, 2023, which means landlords must accept vouchers whether they want to or not.
