In physical therapy, there's a phenomenon called "guarding." After a surgery or a serious injury, the body heals. The tissue repairs. The bone knits. The MRI comes back clean. The surgeon says you're good. But you don't move like you're good. You move like you're still injured — stiff, cautious, holding back. Protecting the thing that doesn't need protection anymore.

I had shoulder surgery in Indore. The labrum, the rotator cuff — the kind of injury where recovery takes months and the surgery itself is the easy part. The shoulder healed. The scans confirmed it. The physio said I had full range of motion. But every time I reached for something overhead, I flinched. Not because it hurt. Because my brain remembered that it used to hurt, and the memory of pain was doing the same job as the pain itself.

This is guarding. The injury is gone but the behavior persists. And it's not irrational — it's the most rational thing your nervous system can do. You got hurt doing that movement. Your brain's job is to keep you alive. So it flags the movement as dangerous, even after the danger is gone, because the cost of one false negative (re-injury) is catastrophic and the cost of a false positive (unnecessary caution) seems low.

Except the cost of unnecessary caution isn't low. It's just invisible.

My mother-in-law had her knee replaced. Same pattern. The new knee was fine — titanium and polyethylene, stronger than the original. The surgeon cleared her. The physical therapist cleared her. She walked like she was on glass. Not because the knee couldn't take it. Because her brain remembered the years of pain that preceded the replacement, and no amount of medical clearance overrides the nervous system's threat model.

The physical therapist's instruction was counterintuitive: "Don't hold back. Don't overdo it — but don't hold back. See if the muscle can bear it. The only way to update the threat model is to demonstrate that the threat isn't real anymore."

That instruction applies to a lot more than knees.

## Revenue Converges to Zero

In my operating history, every time I've hired people to do revenue-generating work, revenue has converged to zero. This is not an exaggeration. It's the data:

Personnel experiments — what actually happened:

  US leasing agents (2017-2018):
    Hired 3. Tour-to-close rate: 53%.
    Revenue: positive.
    Then: inconsistency, no-shows, ego depletion.
    Revenue trended down. Cost stayed flat.
    Net by month 8: barely positive.

  BPO agents, Pakistan (2018-2019):
    Hired through 2 providers.
    Tour-to-close rate: 25% (vs 53% US).
    Revenue generated: zero net.
    Cost per closed deal HIGHER than US agents
    despite 60% lower labor cost.
    Cut both providers.

  Engineering team (2019-2024):
    2 contractors, $4,350/month.
    Last 3 months: zero net revenue generated.
    Twilio incident: five-figure cost, million messages.
    PR reviewer experiment: reviews caught nothing.
    Screaming matches about accountability.
    Terminated.

  Virtual assistants (various):
    Multiple attempts with VAs for admin, follow-up.
    Output quality degraded within weeks.
    Training time exceeded value of output.
    Revenue impact: negligible to negative.

  Every time: start positive (or neutral),
  degrade over weeks/months, converge to zero.
  The pattern is consistent enough to be a law
  of my specific operating environment.
</pre>

After enough of these cycles, I started guarding. Not consciously — I didn't sit down and decide "I will never hire anyone again." I just... stopped trying. When someone suggested hiring a sales rep, I'd feel the flinch. The same flinch as reaching overhead after shoulder surgery. The same involuntary protection of the thing that got hurt.

The AI replaced the people. The margins went to 97%. The system runs overnight. The hooks prevent the disasters. Everything works better without humans in the loop. The evidence supports the guarding behavior. The evidence says: don't hire, it always ends the same way.

But that's exactly what the ACL data shows too. Patients who guard have a 3% re-injury rate on the protected knee vs 6% for patients who don't guard. The guarding "works" — for that specific knee. What it doesn't show is the 4x higher rate of injury on the OTHER knee. The protection creates a new vulnerability.

## What Guarding Looks Like in Business

<pre style="background:#0a0a0a; border:1px solid #222; padding:1.5rem; font-size:0.8rem; line-height:1.6; color:#888; overflow-x:auto; margin:2rem 0;">
The guarding pattern in my business:

  What I'm protecting:
    → Margin (97% contribution margins)
    → Control (I understand every line of code)
    → Quality (hooks > human reviewers)
    → Speed (no coordination overhead)

  What guarding looks like:
    → Won't hire engineers (last team: zero output)
    → Won't hire leasing agents (revenue → zero)
    → Won't delegate to VAs (output degrades)
    → Won't partner on operations (trust deficit)
    → Build everything myself + AI

  What guarding costs:
    → Ceiling on what one person can build
    → Single point of failure (I get sick = everything stops)
    → No one catches my blind spots
    → Can't scale operations that need physical presence
    → Ticketing still broken because "anyone" is me
    → Buildings not getting called because pipeline needs
      human oversight I'm not providing
    → Revenue left on the table because I can't be in
      every conversation simultaneously

  The shoulder is healed.
  I'm still flinching.
</pre>

The ticketing system is broken. I wrote about this in the 30,000 conversations essay — the CRM is a "write-only database," tickets have 25% correct closure rates, and I can't get anyone to fix it because "anyone" is me. That's a guarding symptom. A functioning organization would have someone — an ops person, a coordinator, even a reliable VA — maintaining that system. I don't, because every time I've put a human on an operational task, the output has degraded to zero.

But the degradation might not be inherent to humans. It might be inherent to the specific humans I hired, at the specific stage of the company, with the specific management style I was using (or not using). The sample size is small. The variable control is nonexistent. Drawing the conclusion "humans always converge to zero in my system" from a handful of bad hires is like concluding you'll re-injure your shoulder because it hurt last time.

Maybe I'm wrong. Maybe the next hire won't converge to zero. Maybe the shoulder can take it now. I'm surely wrong in some areas — the question is which ones.

## The Physical Therapist's Instruction

"Don't hold back. Don't overdo it — but don't hold back. See if the muscle can bear it."

That's the right instruction for the shoulder and it might be the right instruction for the business. Not "hire ten people tomorrow" — that's overdoing it. But also not "never hire anyone again" — that's guarding.

The physical therapy version: test the range of motion gradually. A little more each week. See what the joint can handle. If it hurts, back off. If it doesn't, go further. Update the threat model with new evidence instead of running on the memory of old pain.

The business version: test one person. One specific role. One clear deliverable. See if the output converges to zero or holds. If it holds, test further. If it converges, back off — but learn whether the convergence was about the person, the role, the management, or the system.

The rehab protocol (business version):

  Week 1-2: Identify one task that's bottlenecked
    on me being the only human. Not the most critical
    task — one where failure is recoverable.

  Week 3-4: Test a person on that task. Clear scope.
    Clear deliverable. Clear measurement.
    NOT "manage the CRM." More like "update these
    50 tickets with current status by Friday."

  Week 5-8: Measure. Did the output hold?
    Did it degrade? If it held, expand scope.
    If it degraded, diagnose: was it the person,
    the task definition, or the system?

  Week 9+: Either expand (the muscle can take it)
    or back off (real pain, not phantom pain).
    Update the threat model either way.

  The point is not to hire.
  The point is to TEST whether the guarding
  is protecting something real or protecting
  something that already healed.
</pre>

## The Deeper Pattern

McRaney writes about ego depletion — willpower as a finite resource. I've written about that elsewhere, about how hooks compensate for depleted willpower. But guarding is a different phenomenon. Ego depletion is about capacity. Guarding is about fear.

Ego depletion says: "I can't check this deployment because I'm exhausted."
Guarding says: "I won't hire someone to check this deployment because the last person I hired caused a million-message disaster."

The hook solves ego depletion. It runs whether I'm depleted or not. But the hook doesn't solve guarding. The hook compensates for MY limitations. Guarding prevents me from testing whether OTHER people's limitations can be compensated for too.

What if the engineering team had had the hooks? What if the PR reviewers had had the automated fresh-eyes system? What if the leasing agents had had the conversation engine handling 80% of the work and the human just handled the tours and calls? Those systems didn't exist when I had humans. They exist now. The environment has changed. The shoulder has healed. But the flinch persists.

The Bhagavad Gita's instruction — action without attachment to outcome — applies here too. Don't hire because you're sure it will work. Don't refuse to hire because you're sure it won't. Act without attachment to the outcome. Run the experiment. Read the data. Update the model.

Don't overdo it. But don't hold back.

See if the muscle can bear it.
